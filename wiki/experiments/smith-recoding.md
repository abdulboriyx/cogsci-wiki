# Sidney Smith's Recoding Experiment (1954)

## What was tested
The experiment tested whether human immediate memory capacity could be increased by "recoding" information into larger, more informational units (chunks).

## Design and Methodology
- **Participants:** Subjects, including Sidney Smith himself, were trained to recode binary digits (0 and 1) into more complex schemes:
  - 2:1 recoding (binary to quaternary)
  - 3:1 recoding (binary to octal)
  - 4:1 recoding (binary to hexadecimal)
  - 5:1 recoding (binary to base-32)
- **Task:** Participants were presented with long sequences of binary digits and asked to recall them using the learned recoding schemes.

## Results
- **Memory Span Increase:** Without recoding, the span for binary digits was about 9. With 2:1 recoding, it rose to about 18 binary digits (recalled as 9 chunks). With 3:1 recoding, it rose to 27 binary digits (recalled as 9 chunks).
- **Limit of Practice:** Smith himself was able to recall 40 binary digits after extensive practice with 4:1 and 5:1 recoding schemes.

## Replications and Failures to Replicate
- **General Principle:** The principle that recoding increases memory capacity has been widely replicated and is a core concept in cognitive psychology.
- **Specifics:** The ease of recoding depends on the familiarity of the recoding scheme and the amount of practice.

## Theoretical Implications
- **Chunks vs. Bits:** The experiment provided strong evidence for Miller's claim that immediate memory is limited by the number of chunks (7 ± 2), not the number of bits. By increasing the bits per chunk, the total information capacity is expanded.

# The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two: Some Limits on our Capacity for Processing Information

**Author:** George A. Miller  
**Year:** 1956  
**Source URL:** [http://psychclassics.yorku.ca/Miller/](http://psychclassics.yorku.ca/Miller/)

## Overview
George Miller's seminal paper explores the constraints of human information processing. He identifies a consistent limit in our ability to distinguish unidimensional stimuli and our capacity for immediate recall, famously dubbed the "magical number seven, plus or minus two." The paper is a foundational text in cognitive psychology, bridging the gap between information theory (bits) and psychological observation (chunks).

## Core Argument
The human capacity for processing information is limited by a fixed number of "chunks" rather than a fixed amount of information in bits. While our channel capacity for absolute judgment of unidimensional stimuli is around 2.6 bits (about 6.5 categories), our immediate memory span is limited to approximately seven units, regardless of their informational density, provided they are organized into meaningful "chunks."

## Key Findings

### Span of Absolute Judgment (Unidimensional Stimuli)
- **Finding:** Humans can accurately distinguish between approximately 4 to 15 discrete categories (averaging around 6.5) along a single stimulus dimension before errors become frequent.
- **Methodology:** Experiments in absolute judgment where observers assign numerals to stimulus magnitudes (e.g., pitch, loudness, saltiness) without a comparison standard.
- **Evidence:** Strong (based on multiple independent studies by Pollack, Garner, Hake, etc.).

### Span of Multidimensional Stimuli
- **Finding:** Increasing the number of dimensions in a stimulus (e.g., combining pitch and loudness) increases the total amount of information transmitted, but with decreasing efficiency for each individual dimension.
- **Methodology:** Observers judge stimuli that vary in two or more independent attributes.
- **Evidence:** Moderate (data from Pollack, Klemmer, and Frick).

### Subitizing
- **Finding:** There is a sharp discontinuity in the accuracy of reporting the number of items in a pattern at around seven items. Up to six or seven, accuracy is high; beyond that, observers must estimate.
- **Methodology:** Flashing patterns of dots for a fraction of a second (Kaufman, Lord, Reese, and Volkmann).
- **Evidence:** Strong.

### Span of Immediate Memory (Chunks vs Bits)
- **Finding:** The span of immediate memory is relatively constant (7 ± 2) across different types of materials (binary digits, decimal digits, letters, words), despite the vast difference in the amount of information (bits) per item.
- **Methodology:** Immediate recall of sequences of items.
- **Evidence:** Strong (Hayes, Pollack).

### Recoding
- **Finding:** Humans can increase the amount of information they retain by "recoding" or grouping smaller bits of information into larger, more meaningful units (chunks).
- **Methodology:** Experiments training subjects to recode binary digits into octal digits (Sidney Smith).
- **Evidence:** Strong.

## What this source adds that others don't
It introduces the distinction between "bits" (the unit of information theory) and "chunks" (the unit of psychological capacity). It highlights recoding as a fundamental mechanism for expanding effective memory capacity.

## Limitations and Caveats
Miller acknowledges that the "magical number seven" might be a "Pythagorean coincidence" and that the limits for absolute judgment and immediate memory may arise from different underlying mechanisms.

## Open Questions
- Is there a single underlying process that limits both absolute judgment and immediate memory to approximately seven items?
- How does the complexity of a "chunk" affect the absolute limit of the span of immediate memory?

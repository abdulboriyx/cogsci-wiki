# Information Theory in Psychology

## Theory Statement
Information Theory in Psychology applies the mathematical principles of information theory—originally developed by Claude Shannon for telecommunications—to model human cognition as a communication channel. It seeks to quantify the amount of information humans can receive, process, and store.

## Originators
- **George A. Miller:** Applied information theory to human memory and judgment.
- **Claude Shannon:** Developed the mathematical foundation of information theory.

## Core Claims and Predictions
- **Humans as Channels:** The human mind can be viewed as a communication channel with a measurable [channel capacity](/wiki/concepts/channel-capacity.md).
- **Limitation by Bits:** Early information theory in psychology predicted that the limits of human capacity would be defined in terms of bits of information.
- **Limitation by Chunks:** Miller's (1956) work revised this by showing that while bits are relevant for absolute judgment, memory is limited by the number of [chunks](/wiki/concepts/chunking.md).

## Supporting Evidence
- **Absolute Judgment Experiments:** Consistent limits on the number of categories an observer can distinguish (2.3 - 3.9 bits).
- **Immediate Memory Span:** Consistent span of approximately seven items regardless of bit density (Hayes, Pollack).

## Criticisms and Competing Theories
- **Nature of the Chunk:** The definition of a "chunk" is highly subjective and depends on an individual's prior knowledge, making it harder to quantify than a "bit."
- **Pythagorean Coincidence:** Miller himself suggested that the "magical number seven" might be a coincidence rather than a fundamental constant of the nervous system.

## Current Status
- **Dominant:** The concept of [chunking](/wiki/concepts/chunking.md) remains a cornerstone of cognitive psychology, even if information theory is no longer the primary framework for all cognitive research.
